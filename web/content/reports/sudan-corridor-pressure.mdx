<h2 id="executive-summary">Executive summary</h2>

Sudan remains the clearest operational deterioration case on the current platform snapshot. The score is not driven by a single incident. It is driven by a convergence: route fragility, persistent displacement, and reporting patterns that resemble prior pre-escalation sequences more than stabilization periods.

The central analytical question is no longer whether stress is present. It is whether the system retains enough resilience to keep deterioration compartmentalized. Current signals do not support that assumption.

<h2 id="what-changed">What changed</h2>

- Corridor reliability weakened after a series of disruptions across routes that matter for relief and internal movement.
- Humanitarian pressure remained elevated without a visible reversal in displacement intensity.
- Narrative tone stayed sharply negative, keeping analyst attention on downside rather than de-escalation.

| Signal | Current read | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Corridor security | Severe | Access disruptions widen both civilian and operational vulnerability |
| Displacement stress | Elevated | High displacement raises the cost of even small tactical setbacks |
| Narrative tone | Negative | Persistent deterioration in reporting often arrives before cleaner coded event data |

<h2 id="scenario-view">Scenario view</h2>

### Base case

Localized offensives and access degradation continue without a stabilizing diplomatic break.

### Escalation case

A contested route or urban corridor disruption triggers a sharper displacement wave and raises the confidence of a broader deterioration call.

### Tail risk

Regional spillover through neighboring corridors materially broadens the crisis footprint and changes the policy response environment.
